My Story

My passion for tech started as a child. The "computer room" (a staple of the early 2000s) was my favorite room. I would lose myself for hours in games like Roller Coaster Tycoon or The Sims. As a teenager, tech became something different in my life as my interests shifted to social media. I would be up until 2 or 3 am working on the HTML and CSS of my MySpace page - perfecting it with my favorite colors and coding it to autoplay my favorite songs (cringe!). As a young adult, I studied Fashion Merchandising, and tech became a way to see the future: trend boards, color stories, line sheets, and plan-o-grams. I loved creating 3D visualizations of a display in PhotoShop....

As my career in merchandising progressed, technology became something frustrating in my life. I would work for well-established retailers who were running successful and productive businesses, yet they ran on antiquated technology.

I found that to be the trend in my retail career: smart, business-savvy leaders working with programs that were outdated by at least a decade, or simply not using tech at all and running the business with pen and paper! I would find myself asking the question of "why?" - and it usually boiled down to confusing interfaces that weren't straight forward for the user. It bred an environment of intimidation when it came to technology and the retail world. There tended to be large learning curves because software wasn't intuitive.

I used tech to increase efficiency where I could. I converted as many pen and paper tasks to the computer as possible: scheduling, business acumen, training materials, clienteling. And then I focused on making the tools I created as easy as possible to navigate so they would actually be used. I invested time into communicating and training how the tools worked and asked for feedback on how to make them better. I became the person who could create a training on any topic, and make it in a format that was understandable.

The more opportunity I had to work on projects like this, the better I felt. Creating tools and improving processes/experiences is a task that is energy-building for me. I am a problem solver by nature.

Throughout the Pandemic I witnessed technology in a new way - I wasn't just seeing the generational gaps in how technology was used, but also saw large gaps in social accessibility. Technology is meant to be our blessing, but for certain groups it probably felt more like a curse. From a Trauma-Informed perspective, technology was probably even causing some harm. I realized I had been solving problems on a small scale with technology in my career, but what if there was potential for something bigger? What if I could help make apps that bridged equity gaps or interfaces that made sense to the inexperienced user? What if I could make technology products that were helpful and empowering to marginalized groups?

After experiencing my own trauma, life shifted upside down for me. I looked at what made me happy and I decided to make some big changes in my life. I decided it was time to pursue my passion in tech. I learned to code with SheCodes and had an absolute blast earning my certification in Front End Development! I'm currently learning Python, and having even more fun programming solutions to complex problems. My learning journey is only beginning - and the great thing with this industry is there seems to be endless potential to how much you can learn.

I am particularly interested in Front End Development and User Experience, and helping bridge the gaps in technology use. I believe the best tech products are beautiful to look at, accessible to all, and easy to use from any experience level.
